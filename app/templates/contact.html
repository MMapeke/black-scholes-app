History of the Black Scholes Model

You’ve probably heard before that you can’t predict the market. A wide range of people have tried and it just doesn’t work. The Black Scholes Model, in a simplified manner, works upon this assumption and assumes stocks move in a very predictable, but incredibly unpredictable manner (like air molecules spreading in a room).

Applications

For some individuals/groups, options can be used as a form of insurance or hedging. If company XYZ’s stock drops from $30 to $20 and you own a put option with a strike price of $29, you are losing $1 per share at most. The Black Scholes Model is at times used to calculate how much hedging is needed.

An option can also be used as a way to bet on stock price changes without owning the stock. Imagine company WXY’s stock is trading at $30. If you own a call option for $25, you can purchase this company’s stock $5 per share, creating the opportunity for instant profit.

Hedging

An investment used to protect losses in another investment, usually taking the form of a counter position. Reduces potential risk/loss, and also reduces potential gain.

Model Assumptions:

  • The Model is European (can only be exercised at expiration)
  • The Risk-free interest rate and volatility are known
  • Market is perfect and efficient
  • No dividends are paid out before expiration
  • Stock price follows normal distribution
